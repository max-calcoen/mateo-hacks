Our Mission

The Shelf challenges the notion that people serving life sentences deserve a second chance to reform. To prove their readiness to rejoin society on parole, these prisoners often read books and educate themselves. However, their attorneys struggle to provide these resources for them, giving them the chance for redemption. Breaking societal barriers, The Shelf strives to connect these attorneys with donors and patrons who share their vision, showing the world that the incarcerated are still human beings.

Impact

By 2030, the aging prison population is projected to reach a significant milestone, with one out of every three incarcerated individuals being 55 years old or older. This demographic shift underscores the increasing challenges of managing elderly inmates, including the need for specialized healthcare services. The trend also highlights the long-term consequences of sentences, suggesting a reconsideration of sentencing policies and prison reform to address aging populations.

In the United States, a staggering 200,000 individuals are serving life sentences, reflecting the country's tough stance on crime and punishment. This number represents a significant portion of the prison population and raises questions about the effectiveness and moral implications of life imprisonment. It underscores the urgent need for a dialogue on criminal justice reform, particularly concerning rehabilitation and the potential for second chances.

Nearly half of those serving life sentences in the United States were incarcerated before reaching the age of 25, highlighting a critical issue in the justice system's approach to young offenders. This statistic points to the profound impact of early-life decisions on individuals' futures and emphasizes the importance of intervention and support for at-risk youth. It also calls into question the fairness and potential for reform within sentencing practices, particularly for crimes committed at a young age.
